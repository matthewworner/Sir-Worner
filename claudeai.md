"Non nobis tantum nati" is a Latin motto that translates to "Not born for ourselves alone" or "We are not born for ourselves only."
What it means philosophically:
It expresses the idea that our lives should have purpose beyond our own self-interest. We exist not just to serve ourselves, but to contribute to our family, community, and society. It's about duty, service, and recognizing our interconnectedness with others.
The classical origin:
This phrase comes from the Roman philosopher Cicero, who wrote "Non nobis solum nati sumus" (We are not born for ourselves alone) in his work "De Officiis" (On Duties). It was a core principle of Stoic and Roman civic philosophy.
In heraldic context:
When used as a family motto on a coat of arms, it typically signals that the family values:
Service to others over personal gain
Social responsibility
A sense of duty to community or nation
Generosity and public-mindedness
It's quite a noble sentiment for a family motto - essentially saying "we recognize our obligation to use our position/resources/talents for the greater good, not just ourselves." It reflects an ethos of noblesse oblige (the idea that privilege comes with responsibility).

Origin of Worner/Warner:
Norman roots: The name was brought to Britain during the Norman Conquest of 1066 in the form "Warnier." It's of Germanic origin, derived from the old Germanic personal name combining elements meaning "guard" or "protect" and "army" or "warrior."
Occupational origin: The name may also come from an occupational title - "the warrener" - which was a person who managed a warren (an enclosed area for breeding and keeping game animals like rabbits). This was an important role on medieval estates.
Spelling variations: Over centuries, the name evolved into many different spellings including Warner, Worner, Warnere, Warinar, Warrener, Werner, and others. This was common before spelling was standardized - people often spelled names phonetically.
Interesting tidbits:
The Germanic root "warin" (meaning to guard or protect) shows up in various European names, which is why you see similar names across different countries (Werner in German-speaking areas, for example)
As an occupational surname, "warrener" was actually a position of some responsibility - you were trusted with managing valuable game animals for the lord of the manor
